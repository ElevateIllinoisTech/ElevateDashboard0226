HOW MILESTONE BADGES CONNECT TO THE EMPLOYMENT GUARANTEE

Required for guarantee: All 4 Program Progression badges (Pre-College Pioneer through Elevate Graduate) must be earned. Students must also earn a minimum of 3 Career Preparedness badges and 2 Character Development badges.

Enhanced guarantee: Students earning 15+ total milestone badges receive priority placement support, extended guarantee timeline, and access to premium employer partnerships.

Badge portfolio: All earned milestone badges are displayed on the student’s Elevate profile, shared via Credly digital credentials, and included in career services employer referral packages.

BADGE PROGRESSION LEVELS (L1–L5)

All five core skill tracks follow a unified five-level progression system based on the Dreyfus Model of Skill Acquisition (1986) and Bloom’s Revised Taxonomy (Anderson & Krathwohl, 2001). Each level represents increasing depth of competency, from introductory participation to systemic transformation.

L1 — Foundation

Novice

Basic introduction, participation, and active engagement. Students are exposed to key concepts and demonstrate initial awareness through guided activities.

Bloom’s: Remember & Understand

L2 — Skill Builder

Advanced Beginner

Active pursuit of continuous learning and development across multiple professional competencies. Students apply skills in structured settings and seek growth opportunities.

Bloom’s: Apply

L3 — Proficient

Competent

Demonstrated competency and practical application in real-world or professional contexts. Students independently solve problems and produce quality work products.

Bloom’s: Analyze

L4 — Advanced

Proficient

Teaching others and leading initiatives. Students mentor peers, facilitate workshops, and demonstrate mastery through leadership of skill-based projects.

Bloom’s: Evaluate

L5 — Elevator

Expert

Bloom’s: Create

QUANTUM INDUSTRY ROLE CATEGORIES (29 ROLES)

Based on Pina et al. (2025), the QISE workforce spans 4 primary categories and 29 distinct roles, derived from 42 interviews across 23 companies. This framework informs Elevate’s quantum career pathway design.

H: Hardware Roles

Building, maintaining, and scaling quantum hardware. Includes technical managers, quantum hardware engineers, cryogenic/vacuum engineers, FPGA/electronics engineers, fabrication specialists, and lab technicians. Primary education: Physics, EE, Materials Science.

S: Software Roles

Developing quantum software, algorithms, and simulations. Includes algorithm researchers, software engineers, compiler developers, application developers, and classical software infrastructure. Primary education: CS, Math, Physics.

B: Bridging Roles

Connecting hardware and software domains. Includes quantum error correction researchers, benchmarking specialists, systems integration engineers, and theoretical physicists. Primary education: Physics, CS, Applied Math.

P: Public-Facing & Business

Non-technical but essential roles. Technical writers, business development, quantum education/outreach, policy advisors, product managers, and patent/IP specialists. Primary education: Business, Communications, STEM + MBA.

Dominant Challenge: Time Management
56% of students (40 of 72) identified time management as their primary challenge. Commuter students and student-athletes reported compounding difficulties. Several students cited procrastination (12%) and balancing competing demands (18%) as related concerns.

KEY INSIGHTS & RECOMMENDATIONS

1. Strong Initial Engagement, Low Repeat. Over 91% of check-ins are first-time meetings, indicating the program successfully reaches students for initial contact but has not yet established regular coaching rhythms. References & Frameworks

Elevate's design is informed by leading experiential learning frameworks from top research institutions. The following references ground the program in evidence-based practices for career readiness and student development.

Recommended Framework Additions
Harvard University
Harvard Graduate School of Education: Pathways to Prosperity
National framework advocating for structured pathways from secondary education through postsecondary and into careers. Emphasizes work-based learning, career navigation, and the integration of academic and career preparation. Directly aligns with Elevate's pre-college through employment guarantee pipeline.
Harvard Business School
HBS Managing the Future of Work Initiative
Research initiative examining how technology, demographics, and globalization are reshaping work and workers. Provides evidence base for skills-based hiring, experiential credentialing, and employer engagement strategies that inform Elevate's badge system and employment guarantee model.
Massachusetts Institute of Technology
MIT Experiential Learning Office (ELO)
MIT's centralized approach to coordinating experiential learning across undergraduate research, internships, community engagement, and global experiences. The ELO's portfolio model and assessment framework inform Elevate's multi-pathway badge progression and competency verification methodology.
MIT
MIT Teaching + Learning Lab: Digital Credentialing
Pioneer in digital credentialing and open badges for higher education. MIT's Digital Diplomas and Open Learning initiatives provide the research foundation for Elevate's digital badge architecture, portable credentials, and skills verification framework.
University of Oxford
Oxford Careers Service: Skills-Based Career Development
Oxford's competency framework maps transferable skills across academic disciplines to career outcomes. Their approach to integrating career development with academic programs, internship preparation, and employer engagement mirrors Elevate's co-curricular integration strategy.
National Association of Colleges and Employers
NACE Career Readiness Competencies
Industry-standard framework defining eight career readiness competencies agreed upon by employers and higher education: Career and Self-Development, Communication, Critical Thinking, Equity and Inclusion, Leadership, Professionalism, Teamwork, and Technology. Core reference for Elevate's five-skill badge system.
Association of American Colleges & Universities
AAC&U High-Impact Practices (HIPs)
Research-validated educational practices that significantly increase student learning and retention: first-year seminars, common intellectual experiences, learning communities, writing-intensive courses, collaborative assignments, undergraduate research, diversity/global learning, internships, capstone courses. Elevate activities are designed to align with multiple HIPs.
Northeastern University
Northeastern Co-op & Experiential Learning Model
Gold standard for co-operative education with 100+ years of practice. Northeastern's model of alternating academic semesters with full-time work provides the evidence base for Elevate's employment-guarantee approach and the value of structured work-integrated learning in undergraduate programs.
Purdue University
Purdue Gallup Index & Career Services Transformation
Longitudinal study connecting experiential learning to long-term career outcomes and workplace engagement. The Purdue-Gallup Index demonstrates that mentorship, applied projects, and deep engagement during college predict post-graduation career satisfaction and economic mobility.
University of Cincinnati
Cincinnati Cooperative Education Program
Birthplace of cooperative education (1906). Cincinnati's model of mandatory co-op experiences integrated with academic curricula demonstrates institutional-scale implementation of work-integrated learning, providing a historical and operational benchmark for Elevate's program-wide approach.
Georgia Institute of Technology
Georgia Tech Career Center: Experiential Education Model
STEM-focused experiential education integrating undergraduate research, internships, and cooperative education. Georgia Tech's approach to aligning STEM career preparation with industry partnerships mirrors Elevate's AWS Academy integration and engineering school partnerships.
David Kolb / Case Western Reserve
Kolb's Experiential Learning Theory (ELT)
Foundational theory describing learning as a cycle of concrete experience, reflective observation, abstract conceptualization, and active experimentation. Elevate's reflection requirements, portfolio evidence, and applied project components are grounded in Kolb's experiential learning cycle.
